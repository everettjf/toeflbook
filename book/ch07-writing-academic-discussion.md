## 1 · What the Discussion task actually tests

The Academic Discussion task gives a professor's forum prompt + 2 student posts. You write a **200-word reply** that:

- **Takes a clear position** (agree with student A / B, or third stance)
- **Adds new reasoning** (not just restating either student)
- **Cites or builds on at least one student's idea** to show engagement
- **Uses academic register** (formal but not stilted; no slang, no contractions)

The AI scorer rewards: **stance clarity, idea novelty, logical structure, and natural academic English**. It penalizes: padding, templates, off-topic riffs, and copying from the prompts.

**The PEEL frame** (works for any prompt without being a template):
- **P**oint — your stance, one sentence, in the first 30 sec of writing
- **E**vidence — concrete example, study, scenario, or analogy
- **E**xplanation — why the evidence supports your stance
- **L**ink — tie back to a student post + close

## 6 · Common pitfalls

1. **200 words is not aspirational — it's the contract.** Under 180 → underweight. Over 230 → wasting time on a clock you don't have.
2. **Don't restate the prompt.** AI penalizes copy-paste. Paraphrase or skip the restatement.
3. **PEEL is a frame, not a template.** Don't write "My point is X. My evidence is Y. My explanation is Z. My link is W." Write **as if having a real opinion** — but cover all 4 functions.
4. **Cite a student post** by name or paraphrase: "Building on Sarah's point about ___..." This single move bumps Task Achievement scores reliably.
5. **Read aloud during the 30-sec re-read window**. Hearing your sentence catches errors your eye misses. (You can mouth silently in the test.)
6. **Don't over-vocabulary.** A perfect simple sentence beats a clumsy showy one. AI rubric punishes misused fancy words harder than absent ones.
